It gives a structural, not derivational, account of the amplitude factors n^{−σ}. This limitation does not invalidate the framework but must be stated.
Attack 2: The Functional Equation Analogy Is Imprecise
The framework claims the functional equation ξ(s) = ξ(1−s) is the 'reality condition' of a null field. For a real field Φ, the reality condition in Fourier space is Φ̂(k) = Φ̂(−k)*. Translating: s → 1−s̅. But the actual functional equation uses s → 1−s, not 1−s̅.
Proposition 9.1  (Precise Statement of the Analogy).
The functional equation ξ(s) = ξ(1−s) and the reality condition ξ(s) = ξ(1−s̅)*
are DIFFERENT equations off the critical line.
On the critical line s = 1/2+it:
  Reality condition: ξ(1/2+it) = ξ(1/2−it)* = ξ(1/2+it)̅ → ξ(1/2+it) ∈ ℝ.
  Functional equation: ξ(1/2+it) = ξ(1−(1/2+it)) = ξ(1/2−it).
  These agree on the critical line: both give ξ(1/2+it) = ξ(1/2−it).
Off the critical line, s = σ+it with σ ≠ 1/2:
  Reality condition: ξ(σ+it) = ξ(1−σ−it)* ≠ ξ(1−σ+it) in general.
  Functional equation: ξ(σ+it) = ξ(1−σ−it).
  These are different unless ξ(1−σ−it) is real, which is not guaranteed.
CONSEQUENCE: the reality condition gives one constraint; the functional equation gives another.
Together, with ξ(s̅) = ξ(s)̅ (Schwarz reflection), they imply:
  If ξ(ρ) = 0 then ξ(1−ρ) = 0, ξ(ρ̅) = 0, and ξ(1−ρ̅) = 0.
  Zeros come in quadruples {ρ, 1−ρ, ρ̅, 1−ρ̅}, or pairs on the critical line.
The analogy is structurally correct (both express a self-conjugacy) but not an identity. The null framework's functional equation discussion (§V.2) should be read as motivated geometric reasoning, not derivation. The rigorous proof remains via Poisson summation (Riemann 1859, Edwards 1974 §1.6).
Attack 3: The Midpoint Claim Is Trivially True (and Therefore Insufficient)
The claim that σ = 1/2 is the midpoint of the two-sided null line reduces to: 1/2 is the midpoint of [0,1]. This is arithmetically trivial and cannot constitute a proof of RH.
The Gap: Midpoint vs Attractor.
TRIVIALLY TRUE: the midpoint of the segment [0,1] is 1/2.
The functional equation reflection s→1−s has fixed point 1/2. This is trivial.
NOT PROVED: that the ZEROS of ζ are ATTRACTED to this midpoint.
The zeros could be anywhere in the critical strip 0 < σ < 1 (consistently with all known results).
The functional equation guarantees that if ρ is a zero, so is 1−ρ̅.
It does NOT guarantee that ρ = 1/2+it (= its own mirror image).
The non-trivial claim: 'null geometry FORCES the zeros to their own mirror images.'
This requires a DYNAMICAL ARGUMENT: why can't a zero drift off the critical line?
Answer: we don't know. That's why RH is unsolved.
What the null framework adds: a REASON why zeros should prefer σ=1/2.
The null condition k·k=0 is a BALANCE CONDITION: temporal = spatial.
A zero at σ ≠ 1/2 would be an UNBALANCED dark state: one side of the null line heavier.
This is a geometric REASON, not a proof. Converting the reason into a proof
requires the Hilbert-Pólya operator. See §IX.2.
IX.2  The Hilbert-Pólya Approach via Twistor Space
The Hilbert-Pólya conjecture states: there exists a self-adjoint operator H on some Hilbert space such that the non-trivial zeros of ζ(s) occur at s = 1/2 + iλ_n where λ_n are the real eigenvalues of H. If H is self-adjoint, its eigenvalues are real by the spectral theorem, and RH follows immediately. This section makes the null framework's most concrete contribution: it identifies the specific mathematical object that H should be.
Definition 9.2  (Positive Twistor Space).
Twistor space ℂ⁴ has a Hermitian form of signature (2,2):
⟨Z, W⟩  =  Z^0̅W_0 + Z^1̅W_1 − Z^2̅W_2 − Z^3̅W_3
The positive twistor space is PT⁺ = {[Z] ∈ ℙ³ : ⟨Z,Z⟩ > 0}.
PT⁺ is a complex 3-manifold. It corresponds to points in the forward tube of
complexified Minkowski space (Penrose 1969). Holomorphic functions on PT⁺
correspond to massless fields of helicity h on Minkowski space (Penrose transform).
[Penrose 1969, J. Math. Phys. 10; Atiyah, Hitchin & Singer 1978, Proc. R. Soc. A362]
Conjecture 9.3  (The Null-Hilbert-Pólya Operator).
Let H₟₞ₗₗ be the operator on the Hilbert space L²(PT⁺, dμ_{Haar}) defined by:
H₟₞ₗₗ f(Z)  =  −i ∑_p log p · (Frob_p ∘ f)(Z)
where Frob_p is the Frobenius operator acting on L²(PT⁺) by the
p-th prime's arithmetic action, and the sum is over all primes p.
If H₟₞ₗₗ is self-adjoint on L²(PT⁺, dμ_{Haar}), then:
(i)  Its eigenvalues {γ_n} are real.
(ii) The Fredholm determinant det(I − H_s) converges to ξ(s)/ξ(0).
(iii) The zeros of ξ(s) are exactly s = 1/2 + iγ_n.
(iv) Therefore RH holds.
Status: Conjecture. The two open steps are:
  STEP A: Define Frob_p on L²(PT⁺) rigorously and prove H₟₞ₗₗ = H₟₞ₗₗ*.
  STEP B: Prove det(I − H_s) = ξ(s)/ξ(0) by explicit computation.
Why this approach is non-circular: twistor space PT⁺ is defined independently of ζ(s). The Frobenius operators Frob_p are defined by arithmetic (they are Galois-theoretic objects). The conjecture is that THESE TWO independently defined structures combine to give ζ. If true, it would be a deep connection between the null geometry of spacetime and the arithmetic of primes.
Step A is closely related to the work of Connes (1999, Selecta Math. 5) and the L-functions programme of Langlands. Connes constructs a C*-algebra of 'adèle classes' on which the zeros of ζ appear as absorption spectrum. The null framework's candidate PT⁺ is a geometric realisation of this adèle class space, with the Lorentz group acting continuously (replacing the discrete adèle action). Whether these are equivalent is an open question.
Step B is related to the Selberg trace formula: for compact hyperbolic surfaces, the spectrum of the Laplacian is related to the length spectrum of geodesics via the trace formula. The analogue for PT⁺ would require a 'trace formula on twistor space' connecting the eigenvalues of H₟₞ₗₗ to the prime lengths log p. This is the non-trivial analytic step.
The Distance to a Proof.
The null-Hilbert-Pólya conjecture is not proved, but it is a WELL-POSED mathematical problem.
It reduces RH to two specific analytic/algebraic questions:
  STEP A: Self-adjointness of H_{null} on L²(PT⁺). This requires functional analysis on twistor space, a well-defined but difficult problem in the theory of operator algebras on complex manifolds.
  STEP B: Fredholm determinant computation. This requires a trace formula on PT⁺ relating the spectral data of H_{null} to the prime distribution. This is the deepest step.
These two steps are more concrete than the vague 'find a Hilbert-Pólya operator'
that has been the state of the art since 1999. The null framework identifies
a SPECIFIC CANDIDATE (H on PT⁺ via Frobenius operators) and SPECIFIC STEPS.
This is the framework's primary mathematical contribution to RH:
not a proof, but a precise, well-posed conjecture that would imply RH if resolved.

IX.4  Attacking P ≠ NP: The Triangle vs Square Argument
The framework proposes: P ≠ NP because the triangle (△) and square (□) are geometrically irreducible (Theorem 2.1). NP problems have △-structure (minimum enclosure, discrete witness); P algorithms have □-structure (self-dual, orientation-aware). The gap is the transfer from geometric irreducibility to computational class separation.
What Is Already Proved
Theorem 9.4  (det vs per = □ vs Non-□).
The DETERMINANT has □-structure. The PERMANENT does not.
  det(A) = Σ_{σ ∈ S_n} sgn(σ) ∏_i a_{iσ(i)}  [signed sum = orientation = □]
  per(A) = Σ_{σ ∈ S_n} ∏_i a_{iσ(i)}         [unsigned sum = no orientation = non-□]
The sign sgn(σ) = ±1 is the ORIENTATION CHARACTER of the permutation.
Geometrically: sgn(σ) = det of the permutation matrix = the □-volume-form evaluated
on the basis vectors permuted by σ.
Consequences:
  det computable in O(n³): Gaussian elimination = LU = □-factorisation (self-dual).
  per is #P-complete (Valiant 1979): no known polynomial algorithm.
  #P ⊆ P iff P = NP (Toda's theorem: P^{#P} = PH ⊇ NP). [Toda 1991]
In null-line language:
  det(A) = the path integral over null configurations WITH the □ phase factor e^{iπ sgn}
  per(A) = the path integral WITHOUT the □ phase factor
  The □ phase makes det factorisable (LU); its absence makes per hard.
[Valiant 1979, Theor. Comp. Sci. 8; Toda 1991, SIAM J. Comput. 20]
The Specific Missing Steps for P ≠ NP
Conjecture 9.5  (Geometric Irreducibility ⇒ Complexity Separation).
WHAT IS PROVED: △ cannot be generated by □ (Theorem 2.1, this paper).
  Formally: D_3 ⁄⊆ D_4 and D_4 ⁄⊆ D_3 as subgroups of SO(2).
  No sequence of □-operations produces a △, and vice versa.
WHAT IS CONJECTURED: this geometric irreducibility transfers to complexity classes.
  Let Φ : NP → {△-type configurations} be the map:
    SAT problem → the minimum-clause hypergraph (△-structured constraint set)
    Graph colouring → the chromatic polytope (△ at each vertex constraint)
    Travelling salesman → the permutation polytope (△ of optimal tour)
  Let Ψ : P → {□-type algorithms} be the map:
    Polynomial-time algorithm → the □-factorisable computation DAG
    (each step is a matrix operation = □-type; LU, FFT, sort are all □-factorisations)
  CONJECTURED: Im(Φ) ∩ Im(Ψ) = ∅
  (No △-type problem is solvable by □-type operations in polynomial time.)
  If the conjecture holds: P ≠ NP.
MISSING STEPS:
  (i) Formal definition of Φ and Ψ that preserves the geometric structure.
  (ii) A theorem that △-type problems require exponential □-operations to solve.
      This requires a CIRCUIT LOWER BOUND derived from geometric irreducibility.
  (iii) Avoidance of the algebrisation barrier (§IX.3).
The Permanent-Determinant Route
The most concrete version of the above conjecture is the permanent-determinant problem, which is equivalent to P vs NP up to ΦP reductions (Valiant-Vazirani, Toda). The GCT programme (Mulmuley-Sohoni 2001) formalises this as a question about representation theory and algebraic geometry:
Definition 9.6  (Permanent vs Determinant, GCT version).
The permanent perm_n(X) is a degree-n polynomial in n² variables.
The determinant det_m(Y) is a degree-m polynomial in m² variables.
GCT problem: show perm_n is NOT an affine projection of det_m for m = poly(n).
An affine projection: perm_n(X) = det_m(L(X)) for some linear map L.
Such L exists for m = 2^n (trivially) but GCT conjectures no poly(n) m suffices.
In null-line language:
  det = □-type (orientation-aware, LU-factorisable, self-dual)
  perm = △-type (unoriented, minimum-enclosure of the permutation group, not self-dual)
  'perm is not a projection of det' = '△ is not generated by □'
  This is EXACTLY the geometric irreducibility theorem (Theorem 2.1), applied to
  the computational setting via the Mulmuley-Sohoni dictionary.
The GCT approach requires: find a representation-theoretic 'occurrence obstruction'
λ such that V_λ appears in the coordinate ring of det-orbits but NOT in perm-orbits.
In null language: find a □-type representation that cannot appear in a △-type orbit.
Current status: GCT has found potential occurrence obstructions (Burgisser-Ikenmeyer 2011) but proving they are genuine obstructions requires deeper representation theory. The null framework provides geometric MOTIVATION (△ ≠ □) for why such obstructions should exist, but does not provide the proof.

Coda
The framework has been compressed to its logical minimum. One object. One operation. Everything else follows.
The null line k·k=0 is light. Three null lines at 120° is the triangle. Four at 90° is the square. The null line closed is the circle. The proof would formalise this necessity. The framework shows what the proof must say; mathematics must still supply the proof.
